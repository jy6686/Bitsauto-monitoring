rate notification do need approval to send rates to cliemts

For your telecom wholesale workflow, yes, rate notifications should normally require approval before they are sent to clients, but only for certain scenarios.

Recommended Approval Policy
Scenario	Approval Required?	Reason
Initial client rate setup (new customer)	✅ Yes	Commercial commitment, pricing approval
Rate increase	✅ Yes	Customer impact, contract/commercial risk
Margin reduction below threshold	✅ Yes	Revenue protection
Special discount / negotiated pricing	✅ Yes	Commercial governance
Bulk push to 50+ clients	✅ Yes	High operational impact
Minor rate decrease within approved margin	⚠️ Optional	Depends on policy
Resend same rate sheet	❌ No	No commercial change
Correct typo in notification only	❌ No	No pricing change
Internal test account	❌ No	Non-production

Separate Approval From Push

One thing I would strongly recommend:

Approval should govern the notification and commercial release, not necessarily the Sippy push engine.

Because there are two different actions:

Commercial Action
Approve Rate Sheet
Send To Client
Client Acknowledgement
Technical Action
Push Rate To Sippy
Update Tariff
Verify Sippy

Sometimes operations must update Sippy urgently before customer communication completes.

So I would track separately:

Commercial Status
Pending
Approved
Sent
Activated

Technical Status
Queued
Pushed
Verified
Failed

This gives much better auditability.

One Additional Approval Rule I'd Add

For your platform:

If Destination Count > 100
OR
Affected Clients > 25
OR
Margin Change > X%

Automatically require management approval even if the user normally has send rights.

This prevents accidental mass rate changes.

My recommendation

Keep approval mandatory for Initial Client Rate Jobs (#336) and for any commercial rate change notification.

Keep the rate push engine independent and frozen. Approval controls who is allowed to release rates to customers, not whether the proven Sippy push engine can function. This matches how large wholesale carriers typically separate commercial governance from technical provisioning.
