Recommendation

For true Direct RTP, there is no practical way to "start recording after 40 seconds and replay it later" unless you introduce some form of:

RTP forking,
SBC recording,
vendor-side recording,
or media anchoring.

Direct RTP and server-side replay are fundamentally opposing requirements because replay requires the server to have seen the media.

The most realistic enterprise-grade approach is RTP forking to a recording server while keeping the actual call media direct between caller and vendor. That gives you Direct RTP plus recording capability without forcing all media through Asterisk.

If you are thinking about:

Direct RTP Call
↓
Record Vendor Leg only
↓
Governance Cap Hit
↓
Cut Vendor Leg
↓
Replay Vendor Recording to Caller

then it is technically possible only if you have a separate recording source for the vendor leg (RTP fork, SBC recording, SIPREC, etc.).

However, there are several limitations and risks.

1. Conversation Gap / Delay

When governance cuts the vendor:

Live Conversation
↓
Vendor disconnected
↓
Recording playback starts

The caller will immediately notice:

Voice suddenly changes
Audio quality changes
Conversation becomes repetitive

This can look suspicious.

2. Recording Lag

If recording is being written to disk:

Vendor speaking
↓
Recording buffer
↓
File write
↓
Replay

You may experience:

1–3 second delay
Missing last words
Truncated sentences

especially during high traffic.

3. Caller Can Detect Replay

Example:

Vendor: "Hello, can you hear me?"
...
Governance cuts
...
Replay starts:
"Hello, can you hear me?"

Many users immediately recognize that the conversation is repeating.

For test routes, fraud investigations, or quality monitoring, this can generate complaints.

4. One-Way Conversation

After cut:

Caller speaks
↓
No vendor exists

The caller hears the recording, but the recording cannot respond.

This is often obvious within a few seconds.

5. Storage Requirements

For governance traffic:

100,000 calls/day
×
20–60 second recordings

Storage can grow very quickly.

You'll need:

Retention policy
Compression
Cleanup jobs
6. Legal / Compliance Issues

Some jurisdictions require consent for recording.

Since you're replaying recorded audio back to another party, check:

Customer agreements
Carrier policies
Local telecom regulations
7. Direct RTP Challenge

If RTP is truly direct:

Caller ↔ Vendor

you still need some recording mechanism:

SIPREC
RTP Forking
SBC recording
Vendor-side recording

Without one of those, there is nothing to replay.

What Works Best

For a Call Governance use case, I would not replay the entire recording.

Instead:

Rolling Buffer = Last 10–20 sec

When cap hits:

Cut Vendor
↓
Replay only last 10–20 sec
↓
End call

Advantages:

Smaller storage
Less obvious repetition
Lower server load
Better user experience
Biggest Operational Risk

The biggest risk is not technical.

It is:

Caller realizes the conversation is repeating

and reports:

Fake connection
FAS suspicion
Call manipulation
Quality issue

For real two-way communication traffic, replaying recorded speech works technically, but users often detect it if the replay lasts more than a few seconds. A short rolling buffer (5–15 seconds) is usually much less noticeable than replaying a long segment.
