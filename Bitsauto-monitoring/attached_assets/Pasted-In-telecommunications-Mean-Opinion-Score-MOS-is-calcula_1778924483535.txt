In telecommunications, Mean Opinion Score (MOS) is calculated either subjectively by human panels or objectively using algorithmic models. Modern telecom tools bypass manual grading and automatically compute MOS by analyzing network impairments like latency, jitter, and packet loss against mathematical evaluation models.Calculating MOS relies on industry-standard methodologies to translate raw data into a score from 1 (Bad) to 5 (Excellent). The approach differs based on whether it is manual or automated:1. Subjective MOS (The Baseline)Historically, MOS originated from human testing environments.The Process: Human participants are placed in controlled listening environments and asked to listen to a series of audio samples.The Calculation: They grade the call on a scale of 1 to 5 based on their perception of the audio quality. The final score is the arithmetic mean of all the ratings collected from the test subjects:\(\text{MOS}=\frac{\sum _{n=1}^{N}R_{n}}{N}\)(Where \(N\) is the number of subjects and \(R_{n}\) is the individual rating).2. Objective MOS (Automated & Real-Time)Because human testing is expensive and cannot be scaled for live traffic, modern telecommunications networks use automated software algorithms.A. Signal-Based Metrics (PESQ, POLQA, ViSQOL)Algorithms like ViSQOL (Virtual Speech Quality Objective Listener) compare the transmitted audio file with the received audio file. By measuring the physical degradation of the signal, the software mathematically predicts how a human would rate it.B. Parametric Models (The E-Model, ITU-T G.107)Telecom equipment and routers often calculate estimated conversational quality (MOS-CQE) without analyzing the actual voice audio, relying instead on network telemetry.The E-Model computes a Transmission Rating Factor (\(R\)) which serves as the foundation of the calculation:\(R=R_{0}-I_{s}-I_{d}-I_{e,eff}+A\)\(R_{0}\): Represents basic signal-to-noise ratio.\(I_{s}\): Impairments occurring simultaneously with the voice.\(I_{d}\): Impairments caused by delay (latency).\(I_{e,eff}\): Equipment impairment factors (which include codec compression and packet loss).\(A\): Advantage factor (compensation if a user finds mobile mobility or access extremely convenient).The \(R\) value (ranging from 0 to 100) is then mapped to the 1-to-5 MOS scale.Typical MOS Ranges & CodecsThe final score directly dictates how the call is perceived by the end user:4.5 - 5.0: Excellent (HD Voice, indistinguishable from face-to-face)4.0 - 4.3: Good / Satisfied (Standard for high-quality VoIP, minor imperfections)3.6 - 4.0: Fair (Noticeable, but acceptable)Below 3.6: Poor / Unacceptable (Distorted, choppy, communications severely hindered)Achieving and maintaining a high MOS score requires careful network management, including implementing Quality of Service (QoS), properly configuring jitter buffers, and selecting optimal audio codecs (e.g., G.711, Opus, or G.729).
